Utilization Rate Utilization rate is how much you owe compared to how much you are allowed to spend on your credit card .

Every month, credit card companies and banks send a report, you can think of it as a snapshot, to the three credit bureaus (usually on the same day every month). For example, if your credit limit is $1000(how much you’re allowed to spend) and on that day you have a balance of $500(amount owed), that means 50% of your credit has been utilized. You want to stay below 11% utilization rate to get the maximum amounts of points from this category.

If you pay off your balance immediately and not wait till the end of the month, you can avoid a higher utilization rate which will improve your score Age of Credit This category averages the ages of all your accounts, credit cards, mortgages, and other loans.

Lenders normally would like to see that you have experience with using credit reliably.

For example, if you have 1 credit card with 10 years of credit and later down the road you decided to get another loan now your average age between the two is now 5 years.

Age of credit is also one of the slowest to improve because it requires the most time.

This is why it’s so important to start when you’re young. As soon as you turn 18 you are allowed to open up your own credit card to begin building history Derogatory marks Sometimes referred to as a hard inquiry. Anytime a company ask for your permission to check your credit worthiness using your social security, it will count towards your score..

Too many hard inquiries will affect your score negatively. Inquires drops off your report after two years meaning your score will recover.

This means that opening too many credit cards in the same month will affect your score negatively in the short run,
but will improve after the two years are up. Total of Accounts Refers to how many credit cards, mortgages, car loans, student loans and any other accounts you have.
